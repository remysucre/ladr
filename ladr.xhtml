5 Is R 2 a subspace of of the complex vector space C 2 ?
No. By definition, a complex vector space is a vector space over C . In other words the scalars in a complex vector space are complex numbers. Because R 2 is not closed under multiplication by a complex number, it is not a subspace of C 2 .
However, we can define a different vector space using pairs of complex numbers: each element in the vector space is a tuple ( c 1 , c 2 ) : c 1 , c 2 ∈ C , but each scalar is a real number. In this case, R 2 is a subspace, because it contains ( 0,0 ) , is closed under addition and multiplication by a (real) scalar.

12 Prove that the union of two subspaces of V is a subspace of V if and only if one contains the other.
Let the two subspaces be U and W . Suppose u ∈ U and u / ∈ W . Then for any w ∈ W , u+w / ∈ W (because otherwise u+w-w=u ∈ W , contradicting the assumption). But since w ∈ W and W ⊆ U ∪ W , w+u ∈ U ∪ W , so it must be the case that w+u ∈ U . Therefore, w+u-u=w ∈ U , and so W ⊆ U .
13 Prove that the union of 3 subspaces of V is a subspace of V if and only if one of the subspaces contains the other two.
Suppose u 1 ∈ U 1 but u 1 / ∈ U 2 and u 1 / ∈ U 3 (if this does not exist, then we have U 1 ⊆ U 2 ∪ U 3 , and by 12 either U 2 ⊆ U 3 and U 1 ⊆ U 3 or U 3 ⊆ U 2 and U 1 ⊆ U 2 ). Then by the same reasoning in 12, for any u 2 ∈ U 2 we must have u 1 + u 2 / ∈ U 2 . We now consider two cases: u 1 + u 2 ∉ U 3 and u 1 + u 2 ∈ U 3 . In the first case, because u 1 + u 2 ∈ U 1 ∪ U 2 ∪ U 3 , it must be the case that u 1 + u 2 ∈ U 1 , therefore u 2 ∈ U 1 . In the second case when u 1 + u 2 ∈ U 3 , we have u 1 + u 1 + u 2 ∉ U 3 . Because u 1 + u 1 + u 2 ∉ U 2 and also u 1 + u 1 + u 2 ∈ U 1 ∪ U 2 ∪ U 3 , it must be the case that u 1 + u 1 + u 2 ∈ U 1 and so u 2 ∈ U 1 . We can repeat the same argument for any u 3 ∈ U 3 , and finally conclude U 2 ⊆ U 1 and U 3 ⊆ U 1 .
24 A function f: R → R is called even if
f( -x ) =f( x )
for all x ∈ R . A function f: R → R is called odd if
f( -x ) =-f( x )
for all x ∈ R . Let V e denote the set of real-valued even functions on R and let V o denote the set of real-valued odd functions on R . Show that R R = V e ⊕ V o .
We shall first show V e and V o are both subspaces of R R . To show a set is a subspace, we need to show it contains the additive identity 0 and that it is closed under addition and scalar multiplication. Both V o and V e contain the constant function f( x ) =0 , and it's easy to check that they are both closed under addition and scalar multiplication.
To show a sum is a direct sum, we need to show the only way to write 0= v o + v e , where v o ∈ V o and v e ∈ V e , is v o = v e =0 . One direction is easy: if v o = v e =0 (the constant function returning 0) then v o + v e =0 . To prove the other direction, suppose v o ≠ 0 . Then there is some x such that v o ( x ) ≠ 0 . To have v o + v e =0 , we must make sure v e ( x ) =- v o ( x ) . But since v o ( -x ) = v o ( x ) and v e ( -x ) =- v e ( x ) , v o ( -x ) + v e ( -x ) =2 v o ( x ) ≠ 0 .
